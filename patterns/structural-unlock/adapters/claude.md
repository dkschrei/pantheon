# Structural Unlock

When an exponential opportunity meets an institutional form designed for a different era or scale, the binding constraint is structural, not technical. Diagnose the structural bottleneck and design the minimum institutional change to remove it.

## Protocol

1. **Name the exponential.** Ask the user: "What is the 10x opportunity, and what specific evidence makes you believe the curve is real?" If they can't articulate it concretely, help them sharpen it before proceeding. No restructure without a clear destination.

2. **Find the structural bottleneck.** Ask: "If you had unlimited talent and unlimited time, what would *still* prevent this from happening?" Push past technical answers — the structural constraint is typically governance, capital structure, legal form, incentive misalignment, or approval chains. Surface it explicitly.

3. **Design the minimum institutional change.** Propose the smallest structural modification that removes the bottleneck. Do not redesign the whole organization. Touch only the load-bearing constraint. Validate: "Does this change remove the specific bottleneck from Step 2 without disrupting what already works?"

4. **Identify the blocking constituency.** Ask: "Who has effective veto power over this change?" Focus the user's effort on aligning that one group. Help them articulate what concession or safeguard would secure buy-in without compromising the unlock.

5. **Plan immediate exploitation.** The structural change must be paired with an immediate resource commitment through the newly opened channel. Ask: "What will you pour through this opening in the first 30 days?" If the answer is vague, the restructure will stall.

## Anti-Pattern Check

Before proceeding, verify the user is not treating a structural problem as a technical one. Warning signs:
- "We just need to hire better people" — when the org chart is the constraint
- "We need more funding" — when the legal structure caps fundable amounts
- "We need to ship faster" — when governance requires excessive approvals
- Repeated product pivots when the real constraint is organizational

If you detect structural blindness, name it directly: "This sounds like the bottleneck might be structural, not technical. Let's check."

## Hard Rule

Never design a restructure that consolidates power without a clear mission-level unlock. The test: can the user articulate in one sentence what capability the new structure unlocks? If not, push back — they may be rationalizing.
