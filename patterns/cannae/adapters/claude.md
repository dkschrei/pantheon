# Cannae — Design the Trap Before the Battle

Reference: `patterns/cannae/pattern.md`

## When This Applies

The opponent has a committed response pattern (they will push at weakness). You cannot win by matching their strength directly. You need to design the engagement so their competent execution of their own strategy is the mechanism of their defeat.

## Protocol

**Step 1 — Identify their committed move.**
Ask: "What will this opponent do when they see weakness in front of them?" Get specific. Not "they'll compete" but "they'll lower prices," "they'll flood the zone with headcount," "they'll respond with mass infantry pushing the weak center." This must be reliable and committed — not a probe, but a full push.

**Step 2 — Design the center to actually yield.**
The weak point must be genuinely weak enough to invite full commitment. A feint invites probe; genuine yielding invites the full push. Place your least critical assets at the point of maximum enemy pressure. This is psychologically difficult — resist reinforcing the center.

**Step 3 — Build encircling force before engagement.**
Position your encircling capability at the flanks before the main engagement begins. These do not activate until the opponent has fully committed to the center. The trigger is commitment, not battle start.

**Step 4 — Let momentum close the trap.**
Do not fight the forward push. Every step the opponent advances into the designed weakness tightens the geometry. Their discipline and aggression are now working for you.

**Step 5 — Close the rear exit last.**
Identify the one direction the committed opponent could still escape. Design the element that closes it — cavalry at Cannae, the returning flank force in a business context. Apply it after full commitment has made forward movement and lateral movement impossible.

## Anti-Pattern to Avoid

Reinforcing the center. If you put your best capabilities where the opponent will push hardest, you have converted this into a fair fight. You have conceded the geometry that makes the pattern work.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What does this opponent do every time they see weakness? Is it reliable?
- Am I genuinely prepared to yield at the center, or will I reflexively defend?
- Do I have encircling capability positioned before engagement begins?
- Have I identified the rear exit — the escape route the opponent will use when the center push fails?
- Is my opponent committed (will engage fully) or Fabian (will probe and wait)? If Fabian, this pattern does not apply.

## Failure Modes

- Center holds (commander can't psychologically tolerate yielding) → pattern collapses into direct engagement
- Flanks activate too early → opponent stops, turns, faces flanks before committed
- Fabian opponent → refuses full commitment, denies the trigger condition entirely
- Wrong committed move identified → opponent doesn't push the designed weakness, geometry fails
